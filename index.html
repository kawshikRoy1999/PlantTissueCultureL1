Topic 1 · Foundation

What is Plant Tissue Culture?

Imagine cutting a tiny piece from a plant and growing it into thousands of full plants inside a laboratory. No soil. No insects. No diseases. That is exactly what Plant Tissue Culture does — and it is changing agriculture across the world!

🌱

Pick a healthy plant

Choose the best parent plant with strong growth and no disease.

🧫

Place in a clean bottle

Put the tiny plant piece into sterile media with plant food.

🌿

Baby shoots appear

New shoots and roots begin to grow in the bottle.

🌳

Move to soil

Harden the plantlets and transfer them to pots or fields.

📸 Plant tissue culture lab — plantlets in sterile bottles
🎬 Introduction to Plant Tissue Culture
🌿

What is a Clone?

A plant grown from a single parent through asexual (non-seed) reproduction. All clones are genetically identical — perfect copies of the parent plant!

🔁

Clonal Propagation

Making many identical copies of a plant through asexual methods. Same genes, same quality, same yield — guaranteed every single time.

🧫

Micropropagation

Rapid mass multiplication of plants in a lab. Done on special nutrient media, in sterile containers, under controlled light and temperature.

🧬

In Vitro

Latin for "in glass." Any biological process done in a lab container — not inside a living plant. The opposite is in vivo (inside a living organism).

🎯 The Simple Idea — Anyone Can Understand!

Think of it like this: take a tiny piece of a healthy plant (even a leaf tip!), put it in a special nutrient "soup" inside a clean bottle, and keep it in a controlled room. The cells will multiply and eventually form a whole new plant!

  • No soil needed at the start
  • No insect or weather damage
  • Make thousands of plants from just ONE parent
  • All plants are disease-free and high-quality

Topic 2 · Pioneers

History of Plant Tissue Culture

Three brilliant scientists — working over 100 years ago — laid the entire foundation of plant tissue culture. Let's meet them and understand what each one discovered.

💡

Idea begins

Scientists wonder if cells can grow outside the plant.

🔬

Experiments happen

They test cells in special nutrient media.

🥕

Proof appears

Frederick Steward grows a full carrot plant from one cell.

🏛️

Modern labs use it

Tissue culture becomes a powerful tool for agriculture.

📸 The pioneers of plant tissue culture
🎬 History of Plant Tissue Culture
First Pioneer

Ross G. Harrison (1870–1959)

American biologist — first to successfully grow animal tissue in artificial media outside the body. He proved that cells can survive and multiply without being inside an organism. His work inspired plant scientists to try the same with plant cells!

👨‍🔬 Father of PTC

Gottlieb Haberlandt (1854–1945)

Austrian botanist who proposed the idea that every plant cell can grow into a full plant — he called this totipotency. He also proposed dedifferentiation. Though he never succeeded experimentally, his idea was revolutionary. Known as the Father of Plant Tissue Culture.

🏆 Champion of PTC

Frederick C. Steward

First to grow a complete carrot plant from a single carrot cell! This landmark experiment proved Haberlandt's totipotency theory was correct. He is celebrated as the Champion of Plant Tissue Culture.

"Every plant cell has the complete blueprint of the entire plant — it just needs the right conditions to express it."

— Concept of Totipotency, proposed by Gottlieb Haberlandt, 1902

Topic 3 · Core Concept

Totipotency & Dedifferentiation

These two words sound complicated but the ideas are very simple! They explain WHY plant tissue culture is even possible — why a single cell can become an entire plant.

🧬

One cell holds all DNA

Every plant cell carries the full instruction book.

🔄

Cell goes back

Specialized cells lose their old job and become flexible.

🍯

Callus forms

The cells multiply into a soft lump of tissue.

🌱

New plant grows

The callus can turn into embryos, shoots, and roots.

📸 Plant cells under a microscope — each one carries the full plant blueprint
🎬 Totipotency Explained Simply
⭐

Totipotency — What It Means

The ability of a single cell to divide and produce all the different cell types in an organism — including every part of the plant.


Easy analogy: Like a single LEGO instruction booklet that can build the entire castle — every plant cell holds the complete DNA "instruction manual" for the whole plant!

🔄

Dedifferentiation — Going Backwards

When a specialized cell (like a leaf cell) goes backwards to become a simple, unspecialized cell again.


Easy analogy: Like a trained doctor who goes back to being a first-year medical student — the cell "forgets" its job and becomes flexible again!

🧫

Callus — What Forms Next

After dedifferentiation, cells multiply into a callus — a soft, unorganized lump of unspecialized cells. This is the raw material scientists guide into roots, shoots, or embryos.

🌱

Somatic Embryo

An embryo that forms from a regular body cell (not from a seed). It develops exactly like a seed embryo and can grow into a full plant — without any pollination!

🎯 The Full Journey — Step by Step

Step 1: Take a plant body cell (Somatic Cell — SC)
Step 2: Cell loses specialization → becomes a Totipotent Cell (TC)
Step 3: Totipotent cells divide → form Embryogenic Callus (EC)
Step 4: Callus develops → Somatic Embryo (SE)
Step 5: Embryo matures → Full, healthy plant! 🌿

Explants — Where It All Begins

An explant is the tiny piece of plant material you take to start tissue culture. The good news — almost any part of a plant can work as an explant!

🌿

Choose plant part

Leaf, stem, root, bud, or sucker can be used.

✂️

Cut tiny piece

Scientists take the small piece that will start the culture.

🧴

Sterilize it

🧫

📸 Plant parts that can be used as explants
🎬 Explants in Plant Tissue Culture
🌿Most Common

Roots, Stems & Leaves

The most accessible plant parts. Used across hundreds of plant species. Easy to collect, easy to sterilize, and widely effective in TC protocols.

🌱Vegetative

Runners, Suckers & Stolons

Suckers (banana), stolons (strawberry), and runners are underground or creeping structures used for propagation of specific crop plants.

🪨Underground

Rhizomes

Horizontal underground stems — found in ginger, turmeric, and bamboo. Popular explant source for spice crops and ornamental grasses.

🌸Reproductive

Pollen, Anthers & Embryos

Parts from flowers or seeds. Used to produce haploid plants (anther culture) or rescue hybrid embryos that won't germinate naturally.

Topic 5 · Techniques

Types of Plant Tissue Culture

Scientists have developed several tissue culture techniques, each suited to different plant parts and different goals. Here are the 7 main types, explained simply.

🧫

Start with tissue

A plant part or cell is taken into the lab.

⚗️

Choose the method

Callus, embryo, meristem, or anther culture may be used.

🌿

Grow under control

The right media and hormones guide the cells.

🌳

Get new plants

The goal is healthy, useful plants for people to grow.

📸 Different culture types in the laboratory
🎬 Types of Plant Tissue Culture
  • 1
    🧫 Callus CultureAn explant is placed on media and its cells dedifferentiate into a callus — an unorganized, unspecialized lump of cells. This callus is the "clay" that can be shaped into roots, shoots, or embryos by changing the hormone balance. Very widely used in commercial TC!
  • 2
    🌾 Embryo CultureThe embryo from a seed is taken out and grown in vitro. Used for hybrid seeds that can't germinate naturally (hybrid inviability). Also used for immature embryos from unripe fruits that would otherwise die before germinating.
  • 3
    🌸 Seed Culture (Orchid Culture)Orchid seeds are as small as dust and have no stored food — they can only germinate with fungal help in nature. In TC labs, orchid seeds are placed on nutrient media and they germinate beautifully. This is how millions of orchids are produced commercially worldwide!
  • 4
    🌿 Meristem Culture — Best for Disease-Free Plants!The growing tip of a shoot (the meristem) is taken and cultured. Since viruses almost never reach the meristem, plants produced this way are virtually virus-free. Used commercially for potato, strawberry, banana, sugarcane, and many other crops.
  • 5
    🔬 Protoplast Culture — Cell Fusion!The cell wall is removed using enzymes, leaving a "naked cell" called a protoplast. Two protoplasts from different species can be fused to create hybrid cells, which grow into new hybrid plants! Used in genetic improvement programs worldwide.
  • 6
    🌱 Organ CultureAn entire organ — root, stem, leaf, or flower — is cultured while keeping its original structure and function. Used for studying organ development, biochemistry, and preserving the natural architecture of plant organs.
  • 7
    🌼 Pollen / Anther Culture — Haploid Plants!Pollen grains or anthers are cultured to produce haploid plants — plants with only half the normal chromosome number. These are then chemically doubled to create fully homozygous plants — very valuable in plant breeding for rapid variety development.

Topic 6 · Nutrition

Plant Tissue Culture Media

Culture media is the special "food" that nourishes plant cells in the lab. Without the right media, nothing grows! Think of it as a perfectly balanced meal — carefully designed for plant cells.

🧂

Collect ingredients

Scientists measure minerals, vitamins, sugar, and agar.

⚗️

Mix the media

The ingredients are dissolved in clean water.

📏

Set the pH

The acidity is adjusted so the plant cells can use it.

🧫

Ready to feed plants

The media is sterilized and used for tissue culture.

📸 Preparing and dispensing culture media in the laboratory
🎬 How Culture Media is Prepared
⚗️

Macronutrients

Large-quantity minerals — Nitrogen (N), Phosphorus (P), Potassium (K), Calcium (Ca), Magnesium (Mg), Sulfur (S). Essential building blocks for all cell functions.

🔩

Micronutrients

Small-quantity trace minerals — Iron, Manganese, Zinc, Copper, Boron, Molybdenum. Needed in tiny amounts but critical for enzyme activity.

🍬

Sugar (Carbon Source)

Usually sucrose (3%). Since cultured cells can't photosynthesize well inside sealed bottles, they need ready-made sugar as energy!

💊

Vitamins

Thiamine (B1), Pyridoxine (B6), Nicotinic acid. Support cell metabolism and are essential for normal cell division.

🧊

Gelling Agent — Agar

Agar turns liquid media into a firm jelly that cells sit on. Liquid media (no agar) is used for cell suspension cultures.

⚖️

pH Must Be 5.4–5.8

Slightly acidic. Wrong pH stops growth and makes hormones useless. Always carefully measured before media is sterilized!

Topic 7 · Control System

Plant Growth Regulators (Hormones)

Hormones are the "remote control" of tissue culture! By changing the type and amount of hormones in the media, scientists can tell plant cells to form roots, form shoots, or just multiply as callus.

🌱

High auxin

The media tells cells to make roots.

🌿

High cytokinin

The media tells cells to make shoots.

⚖️

Balanced hormones

Equal amounts often make callus.

🌳

New plant grows

Hormones are adjusted until a full plant forms.

📸 Plant growth regulator solutions used in TC media
🎬 Hormones in Plant Tissue Culture
🌱Root Maker

Auxins

High auxin = ROOTS grow!
Examples: IAA, NAA, IBA

Auxins promote root development and cell elongation. IBA (Indole-3-Butyric Acid) is the most commonly used for rooting in banana (1.5 ppm), potato, and many other crops.

🌿Shoot Maker

Cytokinins

High cytokinin = SHOOTS multiply!
Examples: BAP, Kinetin, TDZ

Cytokinins promote cell division and shoot bud development. TDZ (Thidiazuron) at 0.5 ppm + NAA at 2 ppm is the UBKV protocol for banana!

⚖️Equal = Callus

The Golden Rule

Equal auxin + cytokinin = CALLUS forms!

When both hormones are balanced, cells form a callus — unspecialized and undifferentiated. Scientists use this rule to control what the cells become!

📏Elongation

Gibberellins

Promote stem elongation and seed germination. Less commonly used in tissue culture but helpful for elongating dwarf shoots before rooting. Abscisic acid (ABA) promotes dormancy and stress responses.

🎯 Easy Memory Trick for Exams!

🌱 Auxin → Aim for Roots    (High auxin = roots grow)
🌿 Cytokinin → Creates Shoots    (High cytokinin = shoots multiply)
⚖️ Equal amounts → Callus forms (undifferentiated cell mass)

The concentration and ratio depends on the plant species, the tissue used, and the goal of the experiment. Scientists fine-tune this like adjusting a recipe!

🚿

Wash room

Glassware and tools are cleaned first.

🔥

Sterilize room

💨

🌡️

📸 A modern plant tissue culture laboratory
🎬 Lab Tour — All Rooms & Equipment
🧪Room 1

Media Preparation Room

All culture media is weighed, mixed, pH-adjusted, and dispensed into bottles here. Contains: analytical balance, pH meter, magnetic stirrer, distilled water supply.

🔥Room 2

Sterilization Room

Houses the autoclave — uses high-pressure steam at 121°C to destroy all microorganisms in media, glassware, and instruments. Like a super-powered pressure cooker!

💨Room 3 — Most Critical!

Inoculation Room

Contains the Laminar Air Flow (LAF) hood — blows HEPA-filtered sterile air over the workspace. This is where explants are placed into media under complete sterile conditions.

🌡️Room 4

Culture / Incubation Room

Where bottled plants grow on metal racks under controlled light (16 hr/day), temperature (25±2°C) and humidity. This is where multiplication happens!

🚿Room 5

Washing Room

All used glassware — bottles, Petri dishes, flasks — is thoroughly washed and cleaned here before it can be reused. Contamination-free cleaning is essential.

💧Essential Equipment

Double Distillation Unit

Normal tap water has dissolved minerals and microbes. TC needs double-distilled (ultra-pure) water — purified twice — so no impurities interfere with the experiment.

Topic 9 · Cleanliness

Sterilization — The #1 Rule

Contamination is the biggest enemy of tissue culture. Even a single bacterial spore landing on your media can destroy an entire experiment. That's why everything must be sterilized — completely!

🧴

Clean the surface

The explant is washed with chemicals and sterile water.

🔥

Sterilize tools

Forceps and scalpels are flamed or disinfected.

💨

Work in hood

The culture is handled in sterile air.

✅

Contamination free

The bottle stays clean so the plant can grow safely.

📸 Autoclave — the most critical sterilization equipment
🎬 Sterilization Methods in Tissue Culture
  • 💧
    Wet Sterilization — Autoclave (Most Important!)Steam at 121°C, 15 psi pressure, for 20 minutes. Kills ALL microorganisms including heat-resistant bacterial spores. Used for: culture media, glassware, cotton plugs, Petri dishes, distilled water. This is the gold standard!
  • 🔥
    Flame Sterilization — Inside the Laminar HoodMetal tools like scalpels and forceps are dipped in 70% ethanol and passed quickly through an alcohol flame before each use. Done repeatedly during inoculation — every time you make a cut or transfer, tools are re-flamed!
  • 🧪
    Surface Sterilization — For the Explant ItselfThe plant piece is sterilized using chemicals:
    • Tween-20 or Bavistin — removes surface fungi
    • HgCl₂ (0.1%) — powerful bactericide (must be rinsed off!)
    • Sodium hypochlorite (1–2%) — household bleach solution
    • 70% Ethanol — surface disinfection
    After treatment: rinse 3–5 times with sterile distilled water.
  • 🌬️
    HEPA Filtration — Laminar Air Flow HoodThe LAF hood uses HEPA filters that remove 99.97% of particles from air — including bacteria and fungal spores. The entire work surface stays sterile throughout the inoculation process.

⚠️ What Happens If Sterilization Fails?

You'll see white, green, or black fuzzy growth on the media (fungal contamination) or cloudy media (bacterial contamination). The entire bottle must be discarded and autoclaved before disposal. One contaminated bottle can spread to neighboring bottles if not handled carefully. This is why every step is performed with extreme care and precision!

Topic 10 · Step-by-Step

The Micropropagation Process

Micropropagation has 6 clear stages — each one with a specific purpose. Follow this like a recipe: miss a step and the batch fails. Do it right and you can multiply one plant into thousands!

0️⃣

Choose parent plant

Start with the healthiest plant available.

1️⃣

Establish culture

Put the clean explant into sterile media.

2️⃣

Multiply shoots

Many small shoots grow from one starting piece.

3️⃣

Root and harden

Roots form first, then plants move to soil.

📸 The full journey — from tiny explant to healthy plant
🎬 All Stages of Micropropagation
  • 0
    Stage 0 — Choosing the Right Parent PlantSelect the healthiest, most productive parent plant. Sometimes pre-grown indoors for a few weeks to reduce its pathogen load. The quality of your output plants depends directly on the quality of this starting plant!
  • I
    Stage I — Establishing the CultureSurface-sterilize the explant. Inside the laminar hood, cut to the right size and place aseptically on sterile media. Seal and label the bottle. Wait 2–4 weeks for the first signs of growth. This creates the "mother culture."
  • II
    Stage II — Multiplication (The Magic Stage!)The growing culture is cut into pieces and transferred to fresh multiplication media (high cytokinin). Each piece becomes a new culture. Repeat 4–6 times — one plant becomes hundreds or thousands in just a few months. This is where commercial scale-up happens!
  • III
    Stage III — Shooting & RootingIndividual shoots are separated and placed on rooting media (high auxin like IBA). Within 2–3 weeks, roots emerge from the base of each shoot. Now it's a complete plantlet — ready to leave the bottle!
  • IV
    Stage IV — Primary HardeningRooted plantlets are carefully removed from bottles (roots are fragile!), gently washed, and planted in a sterile growing mix in small pots. Kept inside a high-humidity greenhouse initially to prevent drying out.
  • V
    Stage V — Secondary HardeningPlants move to a shade net house with 50% shade for 4–6 weeks, then gradually to full sunlight. After complete hardening, they're ready for open field planting — and they will outperform traditional planting material!

Topic 11 · Transition

Hardening — From Lab to Real World

TC plants are like children who grew up in a perfectly protected environment. Moving them suddenly to the harsh outdoors would be a fatal shock. Hardening is the gentle process of helping them adapt — step by step.

🏠

Start in greenhouse

Young plants stay in a warm, humid, safe place.

🌤️

Get more air

Humidity slowly drops and light becomes stronger.

☀️

Move outside

The plants adjust to sun, wind, and normal soil.

🌾

Ready for field

Hardened plants can survive and grow well outdoors.

📸 Primary hardening greenhouse at UBKV
🎬 Hardening of TC Plants Explained

🏡 Why TC Plants Are So Delicate at First

Inside a culture bottle, plants live in a perfect world:

  • 100% relative humidity — inside a sealed bottle
  • Complete sterility — no bacteria, no fungi anywhere
  • Ready-made sugar in media — no need for photosynthesis
  • Constant, controlled artificial light and temperature
  • Zero wind, zero stress, zero variation

The real world is the complete opposite! Without gradual hardening, most TC plants will wilt and die within 1–2 days of being taken out of culture.

🏠

Step 1 · Primary Hardening

Plants placed in sterile growing mix (Soil : Vermicompost : FYM = 2:2:1) inside a humid greenhouse. Misted frequently. Leaves develop cuticles. Kept for 3–4 weeks.

🌤️

Step 2 · Shade Net House

50% shade cloth filters sunlight. Humidity gradually reduced. Plants develop thicker leaves, stronger cuticles, and more robust root systems over 4–6 weeks.

☀️

Step 3 · Open Field

Full sun, natural humidity, wind, soil microbes — the real world! After 2–3 weeks of exposure, plants are ready for commercial planting at full scale.

📈

Survival Rate

Properly hardened TC plants show 85–95% field survival. Poorly hardened plants may show less than 30% survival. Hardening is never skipped!

Applications in Agriculture

Plant tissue culture is not just laboratory science — it has transformed farming, conservation, medicine, and food production around the world. Here's how it's being used right now.

🍌

Make many copies

One plant becomes thousands of healthy clones.

🦠

Remove disease

Meristem culture helps make virus-free plants.

🌍

Save rare plants

Endangered species can be multiplied and protected.

💊

Support industry

TC helps medicine, breeding, and food production.

📸 Commercial TC nursery — thousands of identical, healthy plants
🎬 Real Applications of Plant Tissue Culture
Most Important

🍌 Rapid Mass Multiplication

Thousands of identical, disease-free plants from one healthy parent in weeks. One TC banana plant → 500,000 plants in one year. Impossible with traditional methods!

Conservation

🌍 Saving Endangered Plants

Rare plants on the verge of extinction are rescued and multiplied in TC labs — a living "gene bank" for plant biodiversity that cannot be replaced.

Biotechnology

🧬 Genetic Improvement

TC cells can be genetically modified to add useful traits: pest resistance, drought tolerance, higher yield, better nutrition. TC is the essential first step for GM crops!

Long-term Storage

❄️ Cryopreservation

TC is the prerequisite for freezing plant material at -196°C in liquid nitrogen. Plants can be stored for decades and revived when needed — a "frozen library" of genetics!

Medicine

⚗️ Biochemical Production

Cell suspension cultures produce valuable plant compounds: alkaloids, flavonoids, taxol (anti-cancer), and other medicines — without needing to harvest whole plants.

Disease Control

🦠 Virus-Free Production

Meristem culture produces plants free from viruses, bacteria, and fungi. The most reliable method for certified disease-free planting material for potatoes, strawberries, bananas.

Topic 14 · Case Study

🥔 Potato TC — Seed Production System

Potato is one of the world's most important food crops. Viral diseases drastically reduce potato yield. TC is the only reliable way to produce certified, virus-free seed potatoes at scale.

🧫

Make clean plantlets

Meristem culture removes disease from the potato line.

🌱

Grow minitubers

Small tubers are formed in protected systems.

🏠

Multiply safely

Protected generations keep the seed healthy.

🥔

Farmers plant them

Certified seed potatoes reach the field.

📸 TC potato plantlets and minitubers — start of certified seed production
🎬 Potato TC & Certified Seed Production
  • G0
    G0 — Tissue Culture StageMeristem culture produces virus-free plantlets. These are multiplied in the TC lab, then transferred to an aeroponics system (roots hang in air, misted with nutrients) or shed net house to produce tiny G0 minitubers — each about the size of a marble.
  • G1
    G1 — First Field Generation (Shed Net House)G0 minitubers are planted in a protected shed net house. Plants are regularly tested for viruses and diseases. The tubers produced are G1 seed — still highly pure and certified disease-free.
  • G2
    G2 — Second Generation (Shed Net House)G1 seed is planted again under shade net conditions. Disease monitoring continues at every stage. G2 seed is still certified for sale to specialized nurseries and seed producers.
  • G3-4
    G3 & G4 — Open Field ProductionFrom G3 onwards, seed potato is grown in open fields. By G4, enough quantity is produced to supply farmers at commercial scale. G4 seed is what most farmers plant in their fields for food crop production.
🚀

Aeroponics System

Potato TC plantlets grow with roots hanging in air, misted with nutrient solution. Each plant produces 5–10 minitubers — highly efficient for G0 production with no soil diseases!

🛡️

Why Virus-Free Matters

Potato viruses (PVX, PVY, PLRV) can reduce yield by 30–80%! TC meristem culture eliminates virtually all viruses, restoring the full yield potential of the variety.

📋

Certification System

Each generation (G0–G4) is inspected and certified by government agencies. Only certified seed potato can be legally sold to farmers in regulated markets.

🌾

Farmer Benefit

TC seed potato gives 25–40% higher yield compared to farmer-saved seed, with better disease resistance and more uniform, high-quality crop.
